Olympic Winter Games and the Cold War

Introduction to the Cold War

Cold War

The Cold War was a political, ideological, social and cultural confrontation that took place between 1945 and 1989 between the Union of Soviet Socialist Republics (USSR) and a group of western countries led by the United States of America (USA).

Effect of the Cold War on Olympic Games

Effect of the Cold War on Olympic Games

The Countries in the USSR block were Bulgaria, Czechoslovakia, East Germany, Hungary, Poland and Romania and, the block led by the USA included Norway, the United Kingdom, France, Denmark, West Germany, Italy, the Netherlands, Belgium, Luxembourg and Greece, also in line with Austria, Ireland and Sweden. Finland and Switzerland remained neutral.

The Cold War implied not only an increase in an arm race between the contending parties, political and social unrest, but also had a strong impact in other aspects of society like the space race and performance in sports and competition. Particularly in sports, the world witnessed how scenarios for healthy competition and rivalry became a weapon for international political affairs.

During the Winter Olympics of 1948 and 1952, the Eastern block had a modest participation. This was a time of economic recovery and austerity for European countries that were vastly destroyed by confrontations during the Second World War. This explains why countries like the United States, Canada, Norway and Sweden obtained a higher number of Olympic victories and medals. Their infrastructure and economy during the war was not widely affected, so economic and social recovery was faster and stronger.

After the Warsaw Pact, the Eastern Block consolidated which caused that Olympic sports in the USSR gained a stellar role in the world. It served not only to showcase their strength as a great nation, but also as a propaganda machine to spread their politics and communist ideas using the games as a platform.

It was in 1956 when the USSR made their big debut in the Winter Olympics, taking home the first place in the medal count. From that moment onwards, and throughout the duration of the Cold War, the USSR and the USA became the biggest potencies in sports worldwide, keeping a constant rivalry and being permanently head-to-head in the medal count in international competitions.

The Cold War came to an end in the 1980s with the fall of the Berlin War in 1989 and the dissolution of the USSR in 1992. However, to this date, the United States and Russia are some of the strongest contenders in international sports competitions, particularly in the Olympic Games.
